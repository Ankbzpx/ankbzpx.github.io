Neural Implicit Octahedral Field (WIP)

WARNING This project is still working in progress (WIP) and results presented here are subject to change

Motivation​

Ever since the badge generation project, I have been fascinated by quad mesh modeling. The edge-loop centered operators, such as loop insertion and loop slide, enable concise and fine-grained control, which is hard to replicate with other geometry representations (i.e. the implicit one). However, such benefit does not come cheap--the mesh not only needs to be quad dominant, its edge loops are also required to align precisely with geometric features, the property that most mesh extracted (i.e. using Marching Cube (MC) or its derivatives) from volume data does not possess.

To bridge the gap, I started with the common practice of extracting mesh first and then applying quadrilateral remeshing. After scrutinizing the source code of Instant meshes, one of the most accessible implementations available in public, I was stunned by how elegant it is. Similar to prior work, it uses a smooth 2D cross field to guide quad tessellation. However, instead of relying on manually specified cross frames, the feature alignment is purely enforced with smoothing--the cross field automatically snaps to sharp features under sufficient smoothness in 3D ambient space. In fact, the initial field is even randomized.

The local smoothness immediately strikes me as a perfect candidate for neural implicit representation, as MLP naturally favors smooth output. However, the cross-field is defined on 2-manifold, and measuring its smoothness requires exhaustive pairwise representation vector matching. Fortunately, its 3D generalization, the octahedral field, paves both obstacles. Being 3D, the octahedral field naturally defines in euclidean space, where its smoothness can be measured in matching-free fashion, thanks to its Spherical Harmonics (SH) parameterized functional representation. Most prominently, the smooth octahedral field in the SH parameterization space inherits the same sharp feature alignment property as its 2D counterpart, making it an idea representation for implicit integration.

Overview​

Given a set of uniformly sampled oriented pointclouds, we leverage MLPs to jointly learn the implicit representation of both SDF and the octahedral field.

MLPsdf: R3→RMLPoct: R3→R9\begin{aligned} \text{MLP}_{\text{sdf}}: & \ \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R} \\ \text{MLP}_{\text{oct}}: & \ \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^9 \end{aligned}MLPsdf​:MLPoct​:​ R3→R R3→R9​

For SDF MLP, we use Siren and follow the official implementation. The octahedral field, on the other hand, is supervised to match the orientation of pointcloud samples, while regularized elsewhere to be locally as smooth as possible. To encourage smoothness, we limit its Lipschitz bound using LipMLP, that compared to the costly minimization of the gradient norm, speeds up training by 50%. Please refer to the publication and the code release for more details.

Neural inverse volume parameterization​

The octahedral field originates in guiding volume parameterization for the hexahedral mesh generation. As hinted in CubeCover, the hexahedral tessellation of the 3D volume is equivalent to regular cube tessellation in its parameterization space. Since we encode zero level set aligned octahedral frame implicitly, apply MC in parameterization space would produce cuts in parallel with cubes’ faces, resulting in quad dominant mesh in original space.

Note that for this application, we can remove the integer grid constrains, or namely, we are only interested in continuous seamless parameterization.

∫V∥∇f−X∥F2 dV\int_{V} \| \nabla f- X \|_F^2 \ d{V}∫V​∥∇f−X∥F2​ dV

where f:V∈R3→Vparamf: V \in \mathbb{R}^3 \to V_{param}f:V∈R3→Vparam​

But before that, it is crucial to examine the singularity graph first. We visualize it as the edges of a bounding tetrahedral mesh following CubeCover.

Cylinder
Model

Our implicit octahedral field gives reasonable singularity graph is R3\mathbb{R}^3R3, but some of them are intricate and noisy that need sanitization. However, even if we successfully cut the singularities and compute inversion-free parameterization, it is still challenging to sample a voxel of coordinates in parameterization space and wrap them back to R3\mathbb{R}^3R3 to query SDF.

Therefore, we take a step back by relaxing the problem to the singularity-free case and tackle the parameterization inversely using a neural field.

∫Vparam∥(∇f−1)T−X∘f−1∥F2 dVparam\int_{V_{param}} \| (\nabla f^{-1})^T - X \circ f^{-1} \|_F^2 \ d{V_{param}}∫Vparam​​∥(∇f−1)T−X∘f−1∥F2​ dVparam​

where f−1:Vparam→V∈R3f^{-1}: V_{param} \to V \in \mathbb{R}^3f−1:Vparam​→V∈R3

Specifically, we model the inverse parameterization function f−1f^{-1}f−1 as a vector potential. The matching between vector potential gradient and the inverse of the octahedral frame is roughly equivalent to the matching between the transpose of the gradient and the original octahedral frame, assuming that the gradient has been regularized with orthogonality. Similar to octahedral field learning, gradient minimization can be performed efficiently using SH parameterization.

The extraction quality is limited but also expected:

  1. We use three separated MLPs to model the SDF, octahedral field, and inverse parameterization function, that align them precisely at zero level set is challenging.
  2. For samples far away from the surface, the SH coefficients deviate significantly from the octahedral variety, such that minimizing the l2l_2l2​ norm no longer guarantees the correct rotation of the canonical frame, leading to inaccurate parameterization. This is especially the case because we directly sample in a parameterization space and have no prior of the wrap position in original space.
  3. The general vector potential modeled by MLP is highly unconstrained, such that encouraging it to have orthogonal gradient is already difficult, not to mention the additional requirement to match the octahedral frame.

However, we believe that this would be an interesting direction for future research.
